## Outline

### The pattern we saw at Double Finance

- We built a fintech product. Support volume kept growing alongside feature count.
- When we categorized tickets, a surprising share weren't bugs or missing features. They were navigation and discoverability problems. Users wanted to do something the product supported, but couldn't get there.
- Examples: "how do I set up a recurring transfer?" (it's in settings → automations → transfers). "Can I export my transactions?" (yes, there's a button on the statements page).

### Why this happens more now

- Teams ship faster. Changelogs get longer. UI surfaces multiply.
- Users don't read release notes. They build a mental model of your product early and stop updating it.
- The result: a growing gap between what the product can do and what the user thinks it can do. Every new feature widens it.

### The standard fixes and why they fall short

- Better onboarding: helps new users, doesn't help existing users who learned the old UI.
- Tooltips and feature announcements: users dismiss them. Banner blindness.
- Better docs and help center: assumes users will search for help before giving up. Many won't — they'll just file a ticket.

### What actually closes the gap

- An interface where users describe what they want to do, and the product does it (or gets them most of the way there).
- This isn't a chatbot that links to docs. It's a copilot that executes the action: navigates to the right page, fills the form, triggers the API call.
- The user stays in control — they confirm before anything irreversible happens.

### Measuring the impact

- Which ticket categories would shrink if users could just tell the product what to do?
- How to categorize your existing tickets to find the "disguised feature requests."
- What this looks like in practice: support volume vs. feature count before and after.
